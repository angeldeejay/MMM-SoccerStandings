\### THE SPLIT



This document lists those European Tier 1 and Tier 2 football leagues that employ mid-season splits to maintain a competitive balance or manage match calendars.



\## European Football League Split Formats (2025/26 Season)

This guide provides a comprehensive breakdown of leagues that divide their table mid-season into Championship and Relegation groups, including specific rules on point carrying and final standings.



\## 1. Core Mechanics of the Split System



Most leagues following this format use a two-stage approach:



* &#x20;Regular Season: All teams first play each other in a standard round-robin (typically 2 or 3 times).
* &#x20;Post-Split Phase: The table is cut (usually at the midway point ) into mini-leagues:
* &#x20;Championship Group: Compete for the league title and European qualification.
* &#x20;Relegation Group: Compete to avoid demotion to the lower tier.
* &#x20;The Glass Ceiling: Once the split occurs, teams cannot leave their assigned half.
* &#x20;A team in the bottom group can finish with more points than a team in the top group but will still be ranked lower in the final official standings.

\## 4. Notable Country Profiles

Romania (Liga I \& II)



Tier 1:

* After 30 rounds, the top 6 enter a double round-robin (10 games), while the bottom 10 play a single round-robin (9 games).
* Points are halved before the split; if a team had an odd number of points, the rounded-up half acts as the first tiebreaker at season's end.

&#x20;

Tier 2:

* Uses a unique three-way split.
* The top six play 10 additional games for promotion, while the bottom 14 are split into two separate groups of seven (Group A and Group B) to determine relegation.



\# Scotland (Premiership)

The SPFL split occurs after 33 games.



* Fixture Challenges: To ensure every team plays 19 home and 19 away games, the league uses "seeding" based on previous seasons' standings.
* This occasionally results in teams playing certain opponents three times at home and only once away.



\# Austria (Bundesliga)

The most aggressive point-reduction system.



* Halving points after 22 games ensures that even a 10-point lead can be cut to 5 overnight, often keeping the title race alive until the final day.



\# Ukraine (Premier League)

* While historical formats included a 6/6 split, the 2025/26 season operates with 16 teams in a standard 30-game double round-robin format without a mid-season split.

\### Promotion/Relegation Play-offs



This section details how the split systems above culminate in Promotion/Relegation Play-offs, where Tier 1 and Tier 2 teams often face off directly to decide who stays up and who goes down.



European Promotion/Relegation Play-off Structures

In leagues with mid-season splits, the end of the season often features a "transitional" phase where teams from different tiers or different split groups meet.



\## 1. The Tier 1 vs. Tier 2 "Battle"

Most of these countries do not use a simple "bottom three down, top three up" rule. Instead, the final spot is often decided by a high-stakes playoff.



| Country       | Tier 1 Position | Tier 2 Position | Position Format|

|---------------|-----------------|-----------------|----------------|

| Germany	| 16th (out of 18)| 3rd (out of 18) | Two-legged home \& away playoff|

| Scotland	| 11th (out of 12)| 2nd, 3rd, 4th | Tier 2 mini-tournament, winner plays Tier 1|

| Austria	| 12th (last)	| 1st Direct swap (no playoff)|

| Belgium	| 13th \& 14th	| 3rd (via playoffs) |	Tier 1 team faces Tier 2 playoff winner |

| Romania	| 13th \& 14th	| 3rd \& 4th |	Two separate two-legged playoffs |



\## 2. Specific Country Breakdowns



\# Scotland:

The "Gauntlet" System - The Scottish Premiership uses a knockout ladder:



* &#x20;   Quarter-final: 3rd in Tier 2 vs. 4th in Tier 2.
* &#x20;   Semi-final: Winner of above vs. 2nd in Tier 2.
* &#x20;   Final: Winner of above vs. 11th in Tier 1 (Premiership).



Note: This heavily favours the Tier 1 team, as the Tier 2 challenger often plays 4 games in 10 days before the final.



\# Romania:

The Mixed Play-off - Because the Tier 1 "Relegation Group" has 10 teams:



* &#x20;   The bottom two (15th and 16th) are relegated instantly.
* &#x20;   The 13th and 14th placed teams enter a two-legged playoff against the 3rd and 4th placed teams from Tier 2.
* 



\# Belgium:

The "Challenger" Path



Belgium’s system is notoriously complex:



* &#x20;   The bottom four of Tier 1 play a "Relegation Play-off" mini-league.
* &#x20;   The survivor of that group often has to play the winner of the Tier 2 (Challenger Pro League) promotion playoffs to secure their spot for the next season.



\# Austria:

The European "Wildcard"



While the bottom team is relegated directly, Austria uses the split to give bottom-half teams a chance at Europe:



* &#x20;   The winner of the Relegation Group (7th place) plays a knockout match against the lowest European-qualified team from the Championship Group.
* &#x20;   The winner gets the final UEFA Conference League spot.





\## 3. Key Rules to Remember



&#x20;   Away Goals: Most European leagues have abolished the away goals rule in these playoffs (matching UEFA's 2021 decision). If tied after two legs, they go straight to extra time and penalties.

&#x20;   VAR: In many countries (like Scotland and Romania), VAR is implemented for the Play-off Finals even if it wasn't used throughout the regular Tier 2 season.
